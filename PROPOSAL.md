## 01 — Where I'm coming from

**Every section on your site has to earn its spot.**

I've seen enough cabinet sites to know the pattern. Some are gorgeous, some are ugly, but the only thing that matters is that they SELL. The average cabinetry site has a homepage stacked with hero sliders, welcome paragraphs, and a stock photo of a kitchen somebody else cooked in. It looks fine, but it doesn't convert.

If a section doesn't qualify, trust-build, or capture: remove it.

## 02 — The split that kills most cabinet sites

**You're selling to two completely different people.**

A cabinet site has to serve homeowners AND contractors, and they want opposite things. Most shops try to build one site for both. It ends up sucking at both.

| They want to know | Homeowner (B2C) | Contractor (B2B) |
|---|---|---|
| Budget range | $3K–$15K, one-time | $5K–$50K+, repeat business |
| What drives the decision | How it looks. How it feels. Can they trust you. | Price. Availability. How fast can they bid it. |
| How long they browse | Weeks. Sometimes months. | Today. They need a quote by 4pm. |
| What "winning" looks like | They fall in love with a look. | They can close their own client fast. |
| What they need on the page | Gallery, inspiration, a designer to hold their hand. | Specs, pricing, stock status, spec sheets. |
| Where you want them to end up | Free design consultation. | Approved pro account. |

**The fork happens in three spots:**
- Utility nav up top: one small link that says "For Contractors" or "Trade"
- Footer: a "Pro Program" link for anybody who scrolls all the way down
- A dedicated /contractors page that does nothing but sell the pro account and gate the portal

The two funnels never merge. The second a contractor sees "Book a free design consultation" they bounce. The second a homeowner sees "Apply for wholesale pricing," they bounce. I build your site so both people feel like it was made for them from the second they land.

## 03 — Your homepage

**You have 8 seconds. Here's how I use them.**

When someone lands on your site for the first time, they're not reading. They're scanning. In about eight seconds their brain decides if you're worth their time. The homepage has to answer four questions before they scroll:

1. What do you actually sell? (Cabinets. Make it obvious.)
2. Who is this for? (Homeowners, with a contractor door clearly visible.)
3. Why should I believe you? (Numbers. Badges. Real reviews.)
4. What do I do next? (One obvious first action.)

**What goes on the page, in order:**

**Hero section** — one real photo of a finished kitchen (not a render, not stock), one specific headline, one primary CTA. No carousels. No "Welcome to our store." If I only get to change one thing on your site, this is where I'd start.

**Trust strip** — a thin row right under the hero. Review count, years in business, certifications, guarantees. Only things we can actually back up. I won't fabricate a review count to pad the number.

**Why us** — 3 or 4 benefit cards that actually say something. Not "Quality products." Not "Great service." Real specifics. "Plywood, never particle board." "Free 1-on-1 designer, no pressure." "Shipped in 3 weeks."

**Style showcase** — for the 30% of visitors who want to browse before they talk to anybody. Each style card shows a real kitchen and a 10×10 starting price. "What does a 10×10 kitchen cost?" is the #1 search term in this entire industry.

**Our process** — 4 numbered steps showing what happens after they sign up. This kills the "I don't know what I'm getting into" fear better than anything else on the page.

**Project gallery, designer spotlight, reviews, FAQ, closer CTA.** Each one pulling weight.

The homepage doesn't need to be clever. It needs to be obvious. Obvious always wins.

## 04 — Product pages

**This is where 60% of your sales are won or lost.**

After the homepage, the product page is the most important page type on the site. It's where the "maybe" turns into a "yes" or a "never mind." Most cabinet sites treat product pages like an afterthought — a photo, a price, a buy button. That's a page built for a t-shirt, not a $12K kitchen.

**What I build into every product page:**

- A real image gallery — main shot in-context (inside an actual kitchen), plus close-ups of door joinery, drawer dovetails, hinge hardware, and the interior
- Color swatches you click, not a dropdown. The main image updates live
- Assembly options (RTA vs. assembled), soft-close upgrades, every option with the price delta right next to it
- A specifications table — contractors screenshot your specs table and send it to their clients. That table becomes your sales tool in their hands
- An "Add to Quote" flow for the people who aren't ready to check out online. People dropping $10K+ rarely click Buy Now
- Reviews with customer photos of their actual install, not stock product shots
- A "Complete the Look" cross-sell so they can add a matching island, crown molding, or hardware without leaving the page
- A sticky mobile bar with the price and Add to Quote button that follows them as they scroll

**Your spec sheet is a sales tool.** When a contractor screenshots it three times to send to three different clients, they're ordering from you forever.

## 05 — The free design page

**One page drives 50% of your revenue.**

If your shop offers free design consultations — and you should — the page that sells that consultation is the single highest-leverage page on the entire site.

**What makes this page convert:**

**Don't put the form up top.** The form comes AFTER they understand what they're getting. Hero first. Then "what you get." Then the designers. Then the process. Then the form.

**Show the designers as humans.** Real headshots, real names, years of experience, one line about who they are. "Book a consult with Kelly" converts meaningfully better than "Book a free consultation."

**Multi-step form, not a wall of fields.** Three steps, broken up. Step 1 is "tell us about your kitchen." Step 2 is timeline and budget. Step 3 is where to send it. Each step feels trivial. By step 3, they're committed. Multi-step forms convert 2–3× better than a single long form.

**Address the fear.** A specific FAQ block answering the questions they're actually asking: Is it really free? Am I obligated? What if I don't like the design? How is this different from just going to Home Depot? These questions kill more conversions than price does.

The free design consultation isn't a marketing gimmick. It's the moat. Home Depot can't offer it. Your cheap online competitors can't staff it. You can — and the page that sells it has to do it justice.

## 06 — The contractor portal

**Where your recurring revenue lives.**

Homeowners buy once. Contractors buy forever. A single general contractor doing 15 kitchens a year is worth more to your shop than 30 homeowner orders, and they're cheaper to acquire because they come back. The contractor portal is how you lock them in.

**Before they sign up: the pro landing page.** A dedicated page at /contractors that does one job: turn a curious contractor into an approved pro account. Qualifying form above the fold (EIN, monthly volume, customer type — filters out homeowners pretending to be pros), public tiered pricing so they see a growth path, contractor-specific benefits (dedicated account manager, net-30 terms, white-label option, free design support), and a spec wall they can download as a branded PDF to use with their own clients.

**The four-tier pricing structure I recommend:**

| Tier | What they hit | What they get |
|---|---|---|
| Bronze | Signup + EIN verified | 15% off retail, account manager, priority ship |
| Silver | $10K lifetime volume | 20% off retail, Net-30 terms, rush ship, free design |
| Gold | $25K lifetime volume | 25% off retail, white-label, showroom access |
| Platinum | $50K lifetime volume | 30% off retail, dedicated design team, co-branded marketing |

**After they log in: the portal itself.**
- Quick reorder — their last 3 orders with a one-click reorder button. Contractors live in this feature
- Active quotes they can revise, share with their client, and convert to an order
- Saved projects / job folders, one per client project, with cabinets, delivery dates, and notes attached
- A resource center with downloadable spec sheets, branded quote templates, and install guides
- A referral program — $200 credit per approved contractor referral. This turns every one of your contractors into a recruiter

## 07 — Trust, proof, and the honest stuff

**You don't buy $12K cabinets from a stranger.**

Every sale you make is a trust transaction. You're asking someone to wire you a five-figure check for something they've never seen in person. The site has to do the work of a showroom, a salesperson, and a reference check, all at once.

**How I build trust into every page:**

- Real photos of real customer installs. Not 3D renders. Not stock. When a reviewer uploads a photo of their finished kitchen, that photo becomes the most valuable asset on your site — more than anything our team creates
- Multi-platform review aggregation. Google, Houzz, Trustpilot, BBB, shown together with counts. Fake reviews cluster on one platform. Real reviews spread across many. Diversity signals legitimacy
- A real About page. Founder story with a real photo. Team grid with real names. A map of where you ship from. Certifications you can click and verify
- A samples page. A $5–20 door sample is the lowest-friction first purchase you can offer, and once a sample is in their kitchen, the conversion to a full order multiplies. Pair it with "spend $20 in samples, get the cost credited to your first full order"
- An inspiration gallery. Top-of-funnel browsers who aren't buying for 2 months live here, and every gallery item links back to the free design CTA

**One thing I won't do.** I will not fabricate review counts, inflate years in business, or invent testimonials to make your homepage look fuller. I've had clients ask. I turn them down. Partly because the FTC has been enforcing hard on fake reviews since August 2024 (fines up to $51K per violation). Mostly because a sophisticated buyer smells fake reviews the way a contractor smells particle board. The second it's caught, every real piece of trust on your site dies with it.

Trust stacks are additive. One badge is decorative. Ten real signals are convincing. Ten fake signals are a lawsuit.
